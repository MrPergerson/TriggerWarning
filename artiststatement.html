Cole Pergerson

Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, and other social media platforms play a significant role in public political debates in the US and around the world. Through these platforms, people reach large audiences just by posting a message or responding to another. This network that we have access to allows us to engage with the political discourse no matter who or where we are. Yet perhaps this freedom has become too burdensome for the social media corporations. Using social media, politicians who are funded and/or influenced by special interests have the ability spread misinformation to a network of followers. In response to the increasingly polarization of the public, platforms tried to limit the power of politicians through means of informing or warning users. My question is whether these efforts work? Are these just bandages for a deeper problem inherit in the design of the platforms? Furthermore, are social media platforms in the position to warn and educate the public about what politicians are saying?
